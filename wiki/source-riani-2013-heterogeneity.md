**Peer-reviewed statistical reanalysis, Statistics and Computing, 2013.** The Springer landing page was fetched this run via the DOI; the full abstract and reference list were extracted and grounded this note (full text gated, but the abstract states the method and finding).

The authors re-examine the twelve individual subsample results behind [[source-damon-1989-nature]]. The abstract reports that these 'show surprising heterogeneity' — more scatter than the quoted counting-statistics uncertainties allow (the 1988 chi-squared homogeneity test was already borderline). Modelling the results by regression on the samples' spatial coordinates across all 387,072 plausible subsample allocations, they 'establish the existence of a trend in the results': measured age appears to vary systematically with position along the sampled strip. They argue better experimental design (multiple, spatially separated samples) would have permitted stronger conclusions.

This is the strongest peer-reviewed support for the sub-claim [[claim-shroud-repair-sample-objection]]: a spatial gradient is consistent with the sampled corner being non-representative (e.g. an admixture of later material). Crucially, the paper does NOT establish a 1st-century date and does not overturn the medieval result for the sampled material — it argues the single-corner sample cannot be assumed representative of the whole cloth. So it disputes the sufficiency of the 1988 sampling, not the measurement itself; it does not support [[claim-shroud-turin-first-century]]. Co-author Fanti is a known Shroud-authenticity advocate, a source of interpretive bias to weigh; co-authors Riani and Atkinson are mainstream robust-statistics methodologists.
