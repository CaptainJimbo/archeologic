**Peer-reviewed article, Nature, 2021 (open access).** Full HTML fetched this run from the Nature landing page via the DOI; abstract, Main text and Results grounded this note.

Primary laboratory dating. The team dated three pieces of wood from Norse contexts at [[site-lanse-aux-meadows]] — each demonstrably worked with metal tools (clean, low-angle cut marks; metal blades were not made by contemporary Indigenous peoples), fixing them as Norse debris. They exploited the AD 993 cosmic-ray radiocarbon spike (a global, single-year Miyake-type C-14 upsurge from a solar particle event) as an absolute anchor in the tree-ring series: once the 993 ring is identified, counting rings out to the preserved waney (bark) edge gives the exact felling year. All three samples yielded a felling year of **AD 1021** — a precise calendar date, not a probability range.

This is the strongest possible support for both [[claim-norse-precolumbian-america]] and the sub-claim [[claim-lanse-aux-meadows-ad1021]]: exactly 471 years before Columbus reached the Caribbean in 1492. The authors frame it as the first securely dated moment Europeans stood in the Americas, and 'the first known point at which humans encircled the globe.' Primary datum (excavated wood + accelerator C-14 + dendro anchor), not an echo.
