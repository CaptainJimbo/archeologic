**Open-access primary study**, fully fetched from PubMed Central. Weber and colleagues (University of Vienna) applied morphological analysis, ancient-DNA sequencing and radiocarbon dating to the cranium and skeleton from the Octagon tomb at Ephesos.

**Grounded findings.** For a century the remains were repeatedly proposed to be Arsinoe IV, daughter of Ptolemy XII and younger half-sister of Cleopatra VII. The fetched text states the connection with Arsinoe IV can be excluded because the individual is genetically male; the cranium is that of an 11-14 year old boy with significant developmental disturbances; radiocarbon dates fall in 205-36 BC; and the genetics suggest an ancestry from the Italian peninsula or Sardinia. The authors conclude the fate of the actual body of Arsinoe IV remains open.

**Relevance to the ancestry debate.** An earlier reconstruction of this skull was widely publicised as showing Arsinoe IV had an African mother, and used to argue that Cleopatra's family, via a shared mother, was partly African. This study removes that evidentiary basis entirely: the skull is not Arsinoe, not female, and not of African ancestry. It therefore supports the framing of [[claim-cleopatra-maternal-uncertain]] (popular substantial-African-ancestry claims are unproven) and indirectly bolsters [[claim-cleopatra-greek-ancestry]] by demolishing a key counter-argument. Limits: the paper is about the misidentified Octagon boy, NOT about Cleopatra's or Arsinoe's own remains, which are unlocated; it disproves a specific claim rather than positively documenting Cleopatra's maternal line.
