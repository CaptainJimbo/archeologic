**The claim.** Etruscan is a non-Indo-European language isolate (with only the fragmentary Raetic and Lemnian as apparent relatives, the so-called Tyrsenian family). How such a language came to be spoken in Iron Age central Italy, surrounded by Indo-European Italic groups such as the Latins, is unresolved.

**Why the genetic result does not settle it.** The archaeogenomic evidence — [[source-posth-2021]] and [[source-ghirotto-2013]] — refutes a recent mass migration of PEOPLE from Anatolia (see [[claim-etruscan-anatolian-origin]]). But Posth et al. explicitly state that the persistence of a probable non-Indo-European language isolate among Indo-European-speaking neighbours is an intriguing and still unexplained phenomenon requiring further archaeological, historical-linguistic, and genetic investigation. Language and genes can decouple: a language can survive or spread without a detectable demic signal.

**The open positions.** (1) The language is a pre-Indo-European survival, in place before the steppe/Italic Indo-European spread and simply not overwritten. (2) It reflects a much older (e.g. Neolithic) connection to the eastern Mediterranean that left no Iron Age genetic trace. (3) Small-scale elite or contact-driven transmission. None is currently decisive.

**Status.** Contested, confidence held at 0.5. This is the correct residue of the investigation: the People question leans refuted, the Language question stays open. It is not evidence FOR Herodotus; it is a genuinely unsolved linguistic-prehistory problem.
