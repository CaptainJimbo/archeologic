**Verdict: REFUTED, confidence 0.12.** The Kensington Runestone — a greywacke slab reportedly unearthed in 1898 by the Swedish immigrant farmer Olof Ohman near [[site-kensington-minnesota]], bearing a runic inscription that describes a party of Goths and Norwegians on a journey west from Vinland and closes with the date 1362 — is judged by the overwhelming consensus of runologists and Scandinavian philologists to be a modern (late-19th-century) forgery, not a genuine medieval Norse artifact.

The decisive evidence is linguistic and epigraphic. The language of the inscription is modern Scandinavian, not the Old Swedish of 1362: its vocabulary, grammar and spelling match 1800s usage, and several words are late loans from German that did not exist in the medieval language. Its runes and numerals include forms unknown to authentic medieval carving — dotted runes, umlaut dots, and pentadic (base-five) numerals — that are hallmarks of 19th-century Scandinavian schoolroom and almanac tradition. As early as 1910 the philologist [[scholar-george-flom]] concluded in [[source-flom-1910]] that the text is a modern inscription in modern runic characters; in 1920 the Scandinavian scholar Rasmus B. Anderson reported in [[source-anderson-1920]] that the examining scholars judged it a clumsy fraud, most likely concocted by Ohman and the itinerant ex-clergyman Sven Fogelblad.

The modern authority [[scholar-henrik-williams]] (Uppsala) reaffirms the forgery verdict on runological grounds; his 2012 survey The Kensington Runestone: Fact and Fiction is the standard modern statement but was paywalled (Project MUSE) during this investigation, so it is recorded here as a lead rather than a fetched source. A small, largely non-specialist minority — chiefly the promoter [[scholar-hjalmar-holand]] and, later, popular authors such as Scott Wolter — continues to defend authenticity, but no professional runologist endorses it. Confidence 0.12 (not 0.0) reflects only residual uncertainty about find-circumstances, not any genuine scholarly doubt: as stated, the claim is settled-false.
