The geared bronze device recovered from the [[site-antikythera-wreck]] (found 1901) and dated c. 150-100 BCE is an analogue mechanical astronomical calculator. It is one of the best-established claims in the history of ancient technology: the surviving fragments physically contain ~30 bronze gearwheels, and independent high-resolution imaging campaigns have read the inscriptions and reconstructed the gear trains.

What it modelled, per the primary decodings: a luni-solar calendar and lunar phases; the 19-year Metonic cycle and, on the back plates, the 223-month Saros eclipse cycle used to predict lunar and solar eclipses (see [[source-freeth-2014-eclipse]]); a mechanization of Hipparchos' theory of the Moon's variable motion via an epicyclic pin-and-slot device (see [[source-freeth-2006-nature]]); a subsidiary dial tracking the four-year cycle of the Panhellenic games (the Olympiad dial); and a front-face display of the positions of the Sun, Moon and the five planets known in antiquity as an ancient Greek Cosmos (see [[source-freeth-2021-cosmos]]).

WORD-SENSE NUANCE: computer here means analogue and mechanical — a geared calculating machine that outputs astronomical positions and cycles — NOT an electronic or programmable digital computer. The device is not evidence of anachronistic technology; it is a sophisticated but fully Hellenistic gearwork instrument, technically unmatched for roughly a millennium afterward.

Provenance of the claim: first framed scientifically by [[scholar-derek-de-solla-price]] (Gears from the Greeks, 1974) from early radiography, then decisively confirmed and corrected by the X-ray CT work led by [[scholar-tony-freeth]] and the Antikythera Mechanism Research Project (2006-2021). Residual scholarly debate concerns internal details — exact gear counts, the precise front-display layout, the calendar epoch, and whether a planetary display was pointer-based or (as some argue) never completed — not the core identification, which is settled. Confidence 0.93: overwhelming, physically-grounded, multiply-replicated support; the small reserve reflects that fine reconstruction details remain open.
