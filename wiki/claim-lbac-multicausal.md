**The claim.** The collapse of the eastern Mediterranean palatial world c. 1200-1150 BCE was not caused by any single agent but by a convergence of stresses acting on a tightly interconnected, fragile system: prolonged drought and climate change, famine, earthquakes, disrupted long-distance trade, internal social and economic strain, and warfare/movement of peoples (including the Sea Peoples). In this reading the Sea Peoples are one symptom among several, not the prime mover.

**Evidence.** Kaniewski et al. 2013 ([[source-kaniewski-2013]]) provide radiocarbon-anchored palaeoclimate proxies from the Syrian coast and Cyprus showing abrupt aridification and climate-driven famine underlying the crisis, and reframe the Sea Peoples as populations displaced by that drought. Middleton 2023 ([[source-middleton-2023]]), reviewing Millek and Jung & Kardamaki, shows the alleged single catastrophic destruction horizon breaks down into fewer, temporally spread, locally-caused destructions, arguing explicitly against a single ultimate cause. This is the framework popularized by Eric Cline's *1177 BC* ([[scholar-eric-cline]]) as a systems collapse.

**Status.** Well supported and close to the current scholarly mainstream, held at 0.7 rather than higher because the *relative weight* of the individual causes remains genuinely contested and some critics warn against simply swapping a mono-causal invasion story for a mono-causal climate one.
