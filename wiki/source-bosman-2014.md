Bosman argues the exodus in the Old Testament is best understood as cultural memory: a remembered past producing collective memories whose relevance did not depend on the historical factuality of the events. He proposes the exodus functioned as a founding myth in the evolving of Israelite and early Jewish identity, and that the scholarly goal should be less about establishing archaeologically verifiable truth claims than about how the memory shaped identity.

This frames the mechanism behind [[claim-exodus-small-kernel]]: even a small or non-literal historical seed could be elaborated over time into the large-scale national narrative preserved in scripture. It supports the contested kernel reading while remaining explicitly agnostic on the literal historicity that [[claim-exodus-historical]] asserts. Author node: [[scholar-hendrik-bosman]].
