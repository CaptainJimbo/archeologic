**The claim.** The ancient Greek historian Herodotus ([[scholar-herodotus]], Histories 1.94) reported that the Etruscans (Tyrrhenians) were Lydians who migrated by sea from Anatolia to Italy after a famine — the classic Eastern-origin or migrationist hypothesis. It was contested already in antiquity: Dionysius of Halicarnassus ([[scholar-dionysius-of-halicarnassus]]) argued the Etruscans were autochthonous, indigenous to Italy.

**What the genetic evidence shows.** Modern archaeogenetics refutes the claim as a RECENT demic migration. The genome-wide time transect of Posth et al. 2021 ([[source-posth-2021]]) found Iron Age Etruscans carried Indo-European-associated steppe ancestry and, crucially, LACKED any recent Anatolian-related admixture; they shared a local genetic profile with neighbouring Latins despite speaking a non-Indo-European language. The earlier mtDNA study of Ghirotto et al. 2013 ([[source-ghirotto-2013]]) independently concluded the culture developed locally, dating the only Tuscany-Anatolia genetic link to at least 5,000 years ago (Neolithic), not the first millennium BCE.

**Verdict.** Refuted as literally stated, confidence 0.25. Two independent genetic methods find no signal of a recent Anatolian/Lydian migration; the Etruscans are genetically largely local. Kept marginally above zero because (a) a deep Neolithic Anatolian-farmer component does exist across the whole region, so an unwary reader could conflate that with Herodotus's story, and (b) genetics constrains people, not language.

**The live nuance.** The refutation applies to the PEOPLE. The origin of the non-Indo-European Etruscan language remains genuinely open — recorded separately as [[claim-etruscan-language-origin]] — because a language isolate persisting among Indo-European neighbours with no matching demic signal is, in the words of Posth et al., still unexplained.
