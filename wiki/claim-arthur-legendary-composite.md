**The reading.** The Arthur of the sources is a legendary figure — a literary construct, possibly a composite of several war-leaders, at most a dim folk-memory — rather than a single identifiable historical person. This is the position most mainstream historians of post-Roman Britain adopt once the late and echo-level sources are set aside.

**Support.** [[scholar-guy-halsall|Halsall]]'s Worlds of Arthur (2014), summarised in [[source-lazarescu-2015]], argues the surviving texts cannot tell us whether a historical Arthur existed and function better as a tool-kit for reading pseudo-history than as evidence for a man. [[scholar-nicholas-higham|Higham]] similarly treats the figure as the product of story-making. The near-total silence of Gildas plus the centuries-late first namings (Historia Brittonum, Annales Cambriae) fit a legend that accreted, not a documented career.

**Dissent.** This is not unanimous. [[scholar-andrew-breeze|Breeze]] ([[source-breeze-2016]]) rejects the mythical reading outright and argues for a historical North-British Arthur; the older Northern-Arthur school (Bromwich, Charles Thomas) leaned the same way. So the legendary reading is well supported but genuinely contested.

**Verdict.** Supported, confidence 0.65: the legendary/composite reading is the majority scholarly view and the best fit for the evidence, while a historicist minority persists. Directly [[claim-king-arthur-historical|disputes]] the bare historicity claim.
