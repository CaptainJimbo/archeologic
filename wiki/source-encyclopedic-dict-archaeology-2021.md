Reference entry, Encyclopedic Dictionary of Archaeology, Springer 2021 (DOI 10.1007/978-3-030-58292-0_160440). Fetched via Springer Nature Link this run; the entry definition was returned in full and grounds this note, though the extended article body sits behind a paywall.

The entry describes the object as mainstream archaeology treats it — a single genuine artifact, no forgery caveat. Verbatim substance: a unique clay disk with stamped inscriptions in a spiral on each face of its 16 cm diameter, found in 1908 at Phaistos, Crete; made of baked clay; signs impressed on the wet clay with a punch or stamp; the world's first typewritten document in the words of John Chadwick (see [[scholar-john-chadwick]]). It records 242 signs in 61 groups, read from the outer edge spiralling inward clockwise.

Crucially for authenticity: the entry states the disk comes from a deposit dated c 1700 BC, which makes it contemporary with the Linear A script — i.e. it is placed by find-context in the Middle Minoan sequence on [[site-minoan-crete]], not treated as an intrusive modern object. This is echo (a reference summary of consensus), not primary excavation data, but it documents that the settled scholarly baseline is authenticity. Supports [[claim-phaistos-disc-authentic]]; does not engage the forgery hypothesis [[claim-phaistos-forgery-hypothesis]].
