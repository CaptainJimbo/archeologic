The claim that Minoan civilization practiced human sacrifice rests on two spectacular but contested finds, both from Bronze Age Crete ([[site-minoan-crete]]).

**Anemospilia (Archanes).** A tripartite building on the slope of Mount Juktas, excavated by [[scholar-yannis-sakellarakis|Yannis and Efi Sakellarakis]] in 1979, was destroyed by an earthquake around 1700 BCE (Middle Minoan II). In its west room lay a young man, apparently bound, on a raised platform, with a decorated bronze blade over his body; a nearby figure may have carried a vessel. The excavators read this as a human sacrifice, perhaps offered to avert the very earthquake that then killed the officiants ([[claim-anemospilia-sacrifice]]).

**Knossos Stratigraphical Museum house.** In a Late Minoan IB house, [[scholar-peter-warren]] and colleagues found the bones of at least four children, about a third bearing fine knife marks consistent with flesh removal, interpreted as ritual sacrifice and possible cannibalism ([[claim-knossos-child-sacrifice]]).

**Why it stays contested.** The osteological data are largely primary and hard to dismiss (see [[source-wall-musgrave-warren-1986]], [[source-musgrave-1994]]), but their meaning is disputed. Critics such as D. Hughes and J. Bremmer (whose works were paywalled or served as JS stubs this run and so are not source nodes) argue the Anemospilia scene admits non-sacrificial readings, and that defleshing at Knossos need not imply sacrifice or cannibalism rather than secondary burial rites. No corroborating pattern of routine Minoan human sacrifice has been established, so two ambiguous cases cannot by themselves settle a civilization-wide practice.

**Verdict.** Genuinely contested. Held at 0.5: the physical evidence is suggestive and difficult to explain benignly at Anemospilia, but the sacrificial interpretation is not secure and no broader pattern is demonstrated. The propitiation reading loosely ties the practice to the seismic and volcanic anxieties of the same era, the world of [[site-akrotiri-thera]] and the Thera eruption.
