**The claim.** If a distinct Dorian people had invaded and replaced the Mycenaean population around 1100 BCE, it should be detectable as an intrusive material-culture package arriving at the collapse horizon and as a genetic discontinuity. Neither is found.

**Material culture.** Judson 2023 ([[source-judson-2023]]) documents that despite a century of searching, no coherent Dorian assemblage appears; the Early Iron Age transition on Crete is regionally varied and better read as internal transformation than as the arrival of a new folk. Candidate intrusive markers once tied to northerners, such as Handmade Burnished Ware, are contested and localized rather than a population-scale signature. The postpalatial world at sites like [[site-mycenae]] shows reoccupation and continuity of local traditions, not a clean cultural replacement.

**Genetics.** The ancient-DNA study of Lazaridis et al. 2017 ([[source-lazaridis-2017]]) finds Minoans and Mycenaeans were genetically similar and that modern Greeks resemble the Mycenaeans with only some additional dilution, which the authors read as continuity but not isolation. There is no signal of a mass replacement event.

**Status.** Supported at 0.72. Held below higher because the genetic sampling does not directly cover the 12th-11th century transition and minor later gene flow is real, so absence of a large signature is not proof of zero movement. This claim disputes [[claim-dorian-invasion-dark-age]] and supports the multi-causal reading [[claim-lbac-multicausal]].
