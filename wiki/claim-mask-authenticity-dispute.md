A separate sub-issue from the (refuted) Agamemnon identification: **is the object as found genuine and unaltered?** [[scholar-david-traill]] and William M. Calder III, in the wider revisionist critique of [[scholar-heinrich-schliemann]]'s reliability ([[source-traill-1989]]), have argued that Schliemann embellished or fabricated discovery narratives, and some have suggested that the most famous mask's unusually 'modern' features (full beard, cut-out ears, defined eyebrows) could reflect modern 'improvement' by a goldsmith, or that Schliemann substituted/altered a find.

**Status: genuinely contested (~0.5).** No metallurgical study has demonstrated forgery, and the mask is generally accepted as an authentic Late Bronze Age object; but Schliemann's documented unreliability keeps the tampering hypothesis alive and unresolved. Critics of Traill (e.g. [[source-easton-1994]]) argue the sceptics overreach. The system does **not** pick a side here: the weight of evidence is roughly balanced between 'authentic, if idiosyncratic' and 'possibly modified', so confidence sits near 0.5. This is orthogonal to [[claim-mask-chronological-gap]] — authenticity does not rescue the Agamemnon label.
