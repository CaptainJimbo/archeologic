Peer-reviewed open-access article, Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences 2009 (DOI 10.1007/s12520-009-0015-2). Full text fetched and stored this run.

USE WITH CARE: the paper's own thesis is fringe — it argues disk side A encodes a Saros eclipse cycle beginning 1377 BCE computed for the Phaistos latitude. That decipherment-style claim is not accepted and this note does NOT endorse it. It is cited here only for its uncontested descriptive and bibliographic content, which it reports reliably.

What it grounds: the disk is a clay object about 16 cm across and 1 cm thick, excavated by Luigi Pernier in 1908 in the ruins of the Phaistos Palace on [[site-minoan-crete]], carrying 241-242 undeciphered stamped signs in roughly 61 boxes over two sides. It compiles the mainstream dating attributions: Arthur Evans 1921 (see [[scholar-arthur-evans]]); Yves Duhoux 1977 dates it between 1850 and 1600 BC; Louis Godart 1995 (see [[scholar-louis-godart]]) dates it anywhere in Middle/Late Minoan. Every cited authority treats the disk as a genuine Bronze Age artifact — none entertains forgery. Mentions [[claim-phaistos-disc-authentic]].
