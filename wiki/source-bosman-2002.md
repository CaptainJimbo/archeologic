Peer-reviewed open-access article (CC BY 4.0), full text fetched and read this run. A scholarly survey of how archaeological discoveries (Amarna letters and the Habiru, the Merneptah Stele, the Egyptian background) have shaped interpretation of the book of Exodus.

Key datum for this investigation: Bosman states that a central element of frustration in the dialogue between archaeology and biblical studies has been the absence of any trace of Israel as a people in Egypt during the Late Bronze or Early Iron Age. This directly undercuts [[claim-exodus-historical]] as literally stated (a mass departure of ~600,000 men).

Bosman also quotes Houtman: the archaeological data have not produced a consensus about the date and nature of Israel's settlement in Canaan, and it is impossible to determine the date of the exodus or reconstruct the Israel of the exodus from Palestinian archaeology alone. This documents genuine scholarly non-consensus and is the reason [[claim-exodus-small-kernel]] remains contested rather than settled.

This is a secondary synthesis (a survey of commentaries and reported finds), not a primary excavation report, so it is weighted as informed scholarly overview rather than a root datum. Author node: [[scholar-hendrik-bosman]].
