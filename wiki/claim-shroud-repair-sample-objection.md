**Verdict: CONTESTED, confidence 0.4.** This is the strongest genuine scientific objection to the 1988 result [[source-damon-1989-nature]]. It has two distinct forms, which must be kept apart.

**(1) Statistical heterogeneity.** The twelve subsample measurements scatter more than counting statistics predict, and [[source-riani-2013-heterogeneity]] (peer-reviewed, Statistics and Computing) reports a spatial trend across the sampled strip. This is real and published: the single corner sample was not demonstrably homogeneous, and better sampling design would have been preferable. Backed by [[scholar-marco-riani]].

**(2) Invisible reweave / repair.** The stronger form claims the corner was a later (16th-century) 'French invisible weaving' repair, so the labs unknowingly dated patch material, not the original cloth (associated with Raymond Rogers and Benford-Marino). This form is weakly supported: [[source-freer-waters-jull-2026]] microscopically examined leftover Arizona sample fragments and found them consistent with the main cloth with no spliced repair yarn or cotton, i.e. no evidence of reweave in the dated material.

Why contested but low-ish confidence: a spatial gradient exists, but no line of evidence turns the sampled linen into a first-century object, and the reweave mechanism specifically is contradicted by direct fragment analysis. The honest scientific position is that the 1988 single-corner sampling was methodologically imperfect and its homogeneity cannot be assumed — but the material actually dated is medieval. This objection therefore qualifies [[claim-shroud-turin-first-century]] without rescuing it.
