The find-spot of the [[claim-kensington-runestone-genuine|Kensington Runestone]]: a farm in Solem Township, Douglas County, west-central Minnesota, near the small town of Kensington. The Swedish-born farmer Olof Ohman reported unearthing the inscribed greywacke slab in 1898, said to have been clasped in the roots of a poplar (aspen) tree while he was clearing land.

No controlled excavation ever documented the discovery, and the stone was for a time used by Ohman as a stepping-stone to his granary before it drew scholarly and popular attention. The absence of a secure archaeological context is one strand of the case against authenticity, alongside the decisive linguistic and runological objections. The stone is today displayed at the Runestone Museum in Alexandria, Minnesota, where it remains a regional tourist attraction and civic emblem despite the scholarly forgery verdict.
