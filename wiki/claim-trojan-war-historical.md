**The popular claim.** Homer's *Iliad* preserves the memory of a real war fought by a Greek ([[site-mycenae|Mycenaean]]) coalition against the city of Troy in the Late Bronze Age.

**What is solid.** There was a real settlement at [[site-troy-hisarlik|Hisarlik]], destroyed more than once in the Bronze Age; the Hittite archives attest genuine Mycenaean-Anatolian friction, and one tablet ([[source-hansen-1997]]) may record warfare in Troy's region c. 1240 BC. A historical *kernel* — some conflict, at that place, in that era — is plausible.

**What is not.** The *Iliad* was composed centuries later (8th c. BC) as oral epic. Skeptics push back hard: [[scholar-frank-kolb]] argues LBA Troy was not even a significant city ([[source-kolb-2005]]), and [[source-schurr-2019]] rejects the [[claim-wilusa-is-troy|Wilusa = Troy]] equation the historical case leans on.

**Verdict.** Genuinely contested. A real event *behind* the legend is plausible; the Trojan War *as Homer describes it* is a literary composition, not documented history. Held at 0.45.
