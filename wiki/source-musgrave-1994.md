Peer-reviewed article, Annual of the British School at Athens, 1994. Fetched via Cambridge Core (DOI 10.1017/S0068245400015306); the abstract and references returned and ground this note. Full text paywalled, so this note rests on the abstract.

In 1987 a Manchester team (J. H. Musgrave, R. A. H. Neave, A. J. N. W. Prag, with E. and J. A. Sakellarakis) made casts of the skulls of the two adults from Anemospilia ([[site-anemospilia]]) whom the excavators identified as the officiating priest and priestess, and reconstructed their faces after medical study. The abstract records that the study found the woman suffered from anaemia. The same facial-reconstruction technique had been applied by this team to the skull from Tomb II at Vergina attributed to Philip II ([[site-tomb-ii]]).

This article does not itself argue for or against sacrifice; it documents and humanizes the individuals in the [[claim-anemospilia-sacrifice|Anemospilia sacrifice]] scene and confirms that the skeletons exist and were studied in detail. It matters here as independent, primary anatomical corroboration of the remains, and as a cross-link: co-authors J. H. Musgrave ([[scholar-jonathan-musgrave]]) and A. J. N. W. Prag ([[scholar-john-prag]]) are the same scholars who reconstructed the Vergina royal skulls, tying the Anemospilia material into the existing Vergina cluster.
