**Verdict: SUPPORTED (settled-true), confidence 0.92.** Norse seafarers from Greenland/Iceland reached and briefly settled the North American mainland around AD 1000, roughly five centuries before Columbus's 1492 crossing.

The decisive physical evidence is [[site-lanse-aux-meadows]] in Newfoundland — the only unambiguously confirmed Norse site in the Americas — excavated from 1960 by [[scholar-helge-anne-ingstad]]. Its material culture (Norse-style turf halls, a bronze ring-headed pin, a soapstone spindle whorl, iron nails and slag from smithing) is diagnostically Norse, not Indigenous.

Dating converges from two independent lab efforts: [[source-nydal-1989]] gives a calibrated radiocarbon range of AD 975-1020, and [[source-kuitems-2021]] uses the AD 993 cosmic-ray radiocarbon spike as a dendro anchor to pin the felling of metal-cut Norse wood to exactly AD 1021 (see the sub-claim [[claim-lanse-aux-meadows-ad1021]]) — 471 years before Columbus.

The Vinland sagas (Graenlendinga saga, Eirik the Red's saga) independently corroborate voyages to 'Vinland,' though as oral histories written down two-plus centuries later they are treated here as leads, not primary evidence. Confidence is held just below 1.0 only because the sagas are secondary and the precise extent/duration of Norse activity beyond L'Anse aux Meadows remains debated; the core claim itself is not scholarly contested.
