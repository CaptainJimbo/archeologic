**Primary ancient source.** Plutarch (c. AD 46-120) wrote within about a century and a half of Cleopatra, drawing on now-lost near-contemporary accounts (including the physician Olympos and Cleopatra's own circle). Fetched as the public-domain 1920 Loeb translation on LacusCurtius (full running text, not a login stub).

**Grounded content (ch. 27).** The fetched text states that Cleopatra could turn her tongue, like an instrument of many strings, to whatever language she pleased, so that she seldom needed an interpreter with Ethiopians, Troglodytes, Hebrews, Arabians, Syrians, Medes or Parthians, and knew the speech of many other peoples too; whereas the kings of Egypt before her had not even made an effort to learn the native language, and some actually gave up their Macedonian dialect.

**Why it matters.** Two load-bearing points for [[claim-cleopatra-greek-ancestry]]: (1) the Ptolemaic house was Macedonian-Greek-speaking (its native tongue is called the Macedonian dialect, and Egyptian is the foreign native language none of them learned), confirming Greek dynastic culture; (2) Cleopatra is singled out as exceptional for her languages, consistent with the tradition that she was the first Ptolemy to learn Egyptian. Note the limits: Plutarch is describing culture and language, not pedigree, so this source speaks to Greek identity and culture, not to the DNA of her undocumented maternal line. Confidence 0.85 for the philological content; the anecdote is rhetorical but the linguistic claim is not seriously doubted.
