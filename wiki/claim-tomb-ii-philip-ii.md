The original and still most widely held identification: the cremated male in the
main chamber of [[site-tomb-ii]] is **Philip II of Macedon**, father of Alexander
the Great, assassinated in 336 BC. Proposed by the excavator
[[scholar-manolis-andronikos]] in 1977–78 on the basis of the tomb's royal
richness, its date, and later the [[claim-tomb-ii-green-cremation|green-cremation]]
and facial-asymmetry evidence assembled by [[scholar-john-prag]] and
[[scholar-jonathan-musgrave]].

**Status: genuinely contested.** The rival reading —
[[claim-tomb-ii-arrhidaios]] — reads the same bones as Philip III Arrhidaios.
The system does **not** pick a side: both identifications have peer-reviewed
forensic support and neither has produced a decisive artifact (no inscription
names the occupant). Confidence is held near 0.5 to reflect a live scholarly
split, not ignorance.

Evidence for is marshalled most fully in [[source-musgrave-2010]]. The strongest
dissent is [[source-bartsiokas-2000]] and, for a different tomb entirely,
[[source-bartsiokas-2015]].
