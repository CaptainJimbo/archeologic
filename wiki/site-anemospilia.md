A Middle Minoan building on the north slope of Mount Juktas near Archanes in central Crete, excavated by [[scholar-yannis-sakellarakis|Yannis Sakellarakis]] and Efi Sapouna-Sakellaraki in 1979. It belongs to the wider palatial landscape of [[site-minoan-crete]] and sits below the Juktas peak sanctuary.

The tripartite structure, probably a shrine, was destroyed and burned in an earthquake around 1700 BCE (Middle Minoan II) and never reoccupied, which sealed its contents where they fell. Four human skeletons were recovered in the collapse, including a young man on a raised platform associated with a large bronze blade. The site is famous almost entirely for the disputed claim that these remains record a human sacrifice interrupted by the quake ([[claim-anemospilia-sacrifice]]).

A place node, not a document. Unlike the palace centres of [[site-minoan-crete]], Anemospilia is a small rural cult building rather than a palace, which is part of why its dramatic deposit is so unusual.
