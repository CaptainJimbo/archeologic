**The claim as stated (mono-causal).** A confederation of migrating raiders, the Sea Peoples, brought down the interconnected civilizations of the eastern Mediterranean around 1200-1150 BCE: the Mycenaean palaces (see [[site-mycenae]]), the Hittite empire, and coastal kingdoms such as [[site-ugarit]]. In this reading the Sea Peoples are the *cause* of the collapse.

**What is actually attested vs inferred.** The distinction matters (see [[claim-sea-peoples-egyptian-attestation]]). The Sea Peoples are genuinely attested — chiefly in Egyptian records, above all the Year 8 reliefs and inscriptions of Ramesses III at Medinet Habu, plus letters from Ugarit. What is NOT securely attested is that they were a single organized force that *caused* the region-wide collapse rather than being one moving part within it. The mono-causal invasion narrative is largely an inference layered onto the Egyptian propaganda imagery by 19th-20th century scholarship.

**Why specialists now reject it as stated.** Two fetched open-access works undercut the mono-causal version. Kaniewski et al. 2013 ([[source-kaniewski-2013]]) present palaeoclimate evidence that the Sea Peoples were themselves people fleeing drought-stricken lands — a *symptom* of the crisis, not its root. Middleton 2023 ([[source-middleton-2023]]) reviews recent work (Millek; Jung & Kardamaki) showing the supposed single, synchronous destruction horizon dissolves on close inspection: destructions are fewer, spread over ~50 years, and locally varied (warfare, arson, earthquake), with no single silver-bullet cause. Eric Cline ([[scholar-eric-cline]]) popularized the multi-causal systems-collapse framing.

**Verdict.** Contested, leaning refuted as a mono-causal claim; held at 0.4. The Sea Peoples were real and did participate in the disturbances, but the evidence does not support them as the sole or primary cause. The better-supported reading is multi-causal: [[claim-lbac-multicausal]].
