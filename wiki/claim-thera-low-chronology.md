The traditional, **archaeologically-derived** date: the eruption falls after the
start of the Egyptian New Kingdom, around 1500 BCE (or a little earlier). It rests
on synchronisms between Aegean, Levantine, and Egyptian material culture —
pottery styles and trade goods found above and below the eruption layers.

It [[claim-thera-high-chronology|disputes the ~1600 BCE radiocarbon date]] by
about a century. Proponents argue the ¹⁴C results are distorted (e.g. by
volcanic CO₂ or calibration-curve plateaus) and that the cross-cultural
archaeology should anchor the date. Held at 0.4: it remains a serious position
defended by respected Aegean archaeologists, but the weight of recent scientific
dating has shifted against it — a genuinely live minority view rather than a
refuted one.
