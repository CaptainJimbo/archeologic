The interpretation that the Anemospilia building ([[site-anemospilia]]) preserves a human sacrifice interrupted by an earthquake, proposed by the excavators [[scholar-yannis-sakellarakis|Yannis and Efi Sakellarakis]] after the 1979 dig.

**The scene.** In the west room, a young man of about 18 lay contracted on a raised platform, his legs positioned as if trussed, with a long decorated bronze blade lying on him. Two other adults died in the same room, and a further person in the corridor was crushed while apparently carrying a vessel. The building, a shrine on the slopes of Mount Juktas, collapsed and burned in an earthquake around 1700 BCE (MM II), sealing the tableau in place.

**The excavators reading.** The bound posture, the blade, and a claimed discoloration of the young man bones (argued to indicate blood loss) led the Sakellarakis team to reconstruct a sacrifice in progress, plausibly an extreme act to propitiate the gods against the impending quake. This narrative reached a wide audience through a 1981 National Geographic feature and the Archanes monograph. The individuals were later studied and facially reconstructed by a Manchester team ([[source-musgrave-1994]]).

**Why contested.** Alternative readings note that the blade may be a votive or grave good, that the bound posture is inferred rather than demonstrated, and that osteological blood-loss claims are hard to sustain. No Minoan text or clear comparandum confirms human sacrifice, so a unique catastrophic context resists a single confident interpretation.

Calibrated at 0.5: a striking, well-documented context whose sacrificial reading is credible but not established. Supports the broader [[claim-minoan-human-sacrifice]]; the disaster-propitiation motive links it thematically to the same seismic and volcanic world as [[site-akrotiri-thera]].
