The interpretation that children were sacrificed and possibly eaten in a Late Minoan IB house at Knossos ([[site-minoan-crete]]), argued by [[scholar-peter-warren]] from bones excavated at the Stratigraphical Museum site in 1979 to 1980.

**The evidence.** A small room yielded the disarticulated bones of at least four children, aged roughly 8 to 12. Of the identified bones, between about 22 and 36 percent bear fine cut marks, which the study ([[source-wall-musgrave-warren-1986]]) reads as knife marks left by cutting flesh from the bones, comparable to marks left when butchering animals. Warren linked the deposit to a ritual context and suggested sacrifice of children with consumption of their flesh, perhaps in a fertility or crisis rite during the troubled LM IB horizon.

**Why contested.** The cut marks and defleshing are hard to deny as physical facts, but their meaning is not. Skeptics argue that defleshing can belong to secondary burial or ancestor rites rather than sacrifice, and that cannibalism is inferred rather than demonstrated. The LM IB destruction horizon was itself a period of upheaval that complicates any single explanation.

Calibrated at 0.45: the primary bone data are strong, but the sacrifice-and-cannibalism reading is one interpretation among several, and slightly less directly supported than the Anemospilia tableau. Supports the broader [[claim-minoan-human-sacrifice]].
