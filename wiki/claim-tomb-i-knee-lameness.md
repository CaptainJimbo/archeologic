The physical evidence underpinning [[claim-tomb-i-philip-ii]]. The male skeleton
in [[site-tomb-i]] displays a severe **knee ankylosis** — the joint fused at
roughly 79° of flexion — pierced by a hole that [[source-bartsiokas-2015]] reads
as a healed penetrating wound. This is matched to the historically attested lance
wound that left Philip II permanently lame in 339 BC.

The inference chain is: pathology → specific historical wound → individual
identity. Each link is contestable — knee ankylosis has other causes, and tying
one lesion to one recorded wound is an argument by resemblance, not proof. But it
is a genuine, striking correspondence and the paper's central exhibit. Confidence
0.5: the observation is real and well documented; the identification it is
marshalled for is not settled.
