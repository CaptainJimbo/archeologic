Peer-reviewed research article, Annual of the British School at Athens, 1986. Fetched via Cambridge Core (DOI 10.1017/S0068245400020207); the abstract and reference apparatus returned and ground this note. The full text is behind a paywall, so this note rests on the abstract and the article own summary of its findings.

The paper publishes the full osteological record of human bones from a small room of a Late Minoan IB house at Knossos (Stratigraphical Museum site). Its central factual claim, stated in the abstract, is that about a third of the bones bear knife marks suggesting that flesh had been cut from them. The endnotes quantify this: of a minimum of 199 identified bones, 79 bear cut marks, giving a maximum marked figure near 36 percent and a best minimum near 22 percent. The remains are largely those of children.

The authors present the stratigraphy and context, the osteological record, an analysis of the skulls and mandibles and their evidence for age, reasons for the treatment of the bodies, and comparative evidence. This is the primary publication underlying [[claim-knossos-child-sacrifice]]. The cut-mark data are primary and strong; the sacrificial and cannibalistic interpretation attached to them is the contested part. Co-author J. H. Musgrave is the same Bristol anatomist ([[scholar-jonathan-musgrave]]) who later worked on the Anemospilia and Vergina skulls, an internal cross-link across the wiki clusters.
