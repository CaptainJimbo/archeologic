**Open-access article (fetched this run; Studia Celtica Posnaniensia, Adam Mickiewicz University, CC-BY-NC-ND; landing page with full abstract retrieved).** [[scholar-andrew-breeze|Andrew Breeze]] replies to a recent discussion of Arthur and Wales.

**Stated argument.** Breeze concludes, in his own summary, that Arthur really existed: he is not a myth or a legend, but historical. He argues Arthur was not Welsh but a North Briton, perhaps a Strathclyder, whose battles against other Britons can be located in southern Scotland and the Borders; he dates Camlann to 537 after the Welsh annals and places it north of Carlisle, while detaching Mount Badon (dated 493) from Arthur. He aligns with Northern-Arthur proponents Rachel Bromwich and Charles Thomas against skeptics such as Kenneth Jackson.

**Bearing on the claim.** This is the minority historicist voice, included for balance. It supports [[claim-king-arthur-historical]] and disputes [[claim-arthur-legendary-composite]] — but note the argument rests on late Welsh sources and locations that are themselves heavily contested, so it does not overturn the skeptical majority.
