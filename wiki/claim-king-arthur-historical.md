**The popular claim.** Arthur was a real 5th-6th century AD war-leader who fought the Anglo-Saxons in post-Roman Britain, the man behind the medieval legend.

**Why it fails as literally stated.** There is no contemporary evidence for such a person. Gildas, writing in the 6th century (the closest source to the supposed events), describes the British victory at Mount Badon but never names Arthur. The earliest texts that name him appear centuries later: the 9th-century Historia Brittonum (attributed to Nennius) and the 10th-century Annales Cambriae. This gap of roughly three centuries between the alleged events and the first naming is the core problem: the namings are echo, not primary witness, and may themselves be legend hardening into pseudo-history.

**Scholarly majority.** Most modern historians of the period conclude the evidence cannot establish a historical Arthur. [[scholar-guy-halsall|Halsall]] argues it is almost impossible to know whether the figure existed at all ([[source-lazarescu-2015]]); [[scholar-nicholas-higham|Higham]] treats Arthur as a literary construct rather than a documented person. The mainstream reading is therefore the [[claim-arthur-legendary-composite|legendary/composite]] one.

**Genuine dissent.** A minority of specialists still argue for a historical Arthur. [[scholar-andrew-breeze|Andrew Breeze]] contends Arthur really existed as a North Briton warlord ([[source-breeze-2016]]) — a live but distinctly minority position that rests on late and much-debated sources.

**Verdict.** Refuted as literally stated: there is no solid evidence Arthur existed as a real 5th-6th century figure. Held at 0.15 — low confidence that the bare claim is true, while acknowledging the question is contested at the margins rather than closed. The defensible reading is [[claim-arthur-legendary-composite]].
