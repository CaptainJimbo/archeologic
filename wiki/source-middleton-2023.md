**Open-access review article (fetched this run, Antiquity, CC-BY).** Middleton reviews Jesse Millek's *Destruction and its impact* (2023) and Jung & Kardamaki's *Synchronizing the destructions of the Mycenaean palaces* (2022), using them to reassess the standard narrative of the c. 1200 BC collapse.

**Core argument.** Both books, Middleton writes, successfully reassess the simplistic and catastrophist characterisation of the end of the Late Bronze Age and provide a more nuanced picture. He notes that whether there was even a *single* collapse marked by a widespread destruction horizon is up for debate, and explicitly rejects the notion of a single destruction horizon with a single ultimate cause or a single silver-bullet cause. Millek accepts destructions only within a generous 1225-1175 BC window and finds several supposed collapse-destructions (e.g. Orchomenos) lack evidence, while attributing others (Midea, Pylos, Tiryns) to warfare or arson of varied and local origin.

**Bearing on the claim.** This is secondary/echo-level literature (a review, not new excavation), but it documents the current scholarly consensus that the mono-causal, catastrophist Sea Peoples story is rejected.
