The gold funerary face-mask found by [[scholar-heinrich-schliemann]] in 1876 in **Shaft Grave V**, Grave Circle A at [[site-mycenae]], is popularly called the *Mask of Agamemnon*, on Schliemann's identification of its wearer with the Homeric king of Mycenae who led the Greeks against Troy.

**Status: refuted (as literally stated).** The attribution fails on chronology: the mask dates to **Late Helladic I, c. 1600–1550 BCE** — see [[claim-mask-chronological-gap]] — some **300 years before** the traditional dramatic date of the Trojan War (~1250 BCE) with which any historical 'Agamemnon' is associated. The man in the grave cannot be Agamemnon; the name is a museum label of romantic convenience, not an identification. Essentially all scholars reject it, and even the source of the claim, Schliemann's own *Mycenae* (1878, [[source-schliemann-mycenae-1878]]), is now judged 'ill-founded' in its conclusions.

Confidence is held very low (0.05), not zero, only because 'Agamemnon' as a legendary figure has no fixed historicity to disprove absolutely — but as an identification of *this* burial it is untenable. Note this is distinct from the separate, genuinely open question of whether the object itself was tampered with: [[claim-mask-authenticity-dispute]]. The popular name is sustained by echo (guidebooks, museum captions), not by any primary evidence.
