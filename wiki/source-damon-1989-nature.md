**Peer-reviewed article, Nature, 16 February 1989 — the primary radiocarbon result.** The landing page was fetched this run via the DOI; the abstract, the full 21-author list, and the reference section were extracted and grounded this note. The full body sits behind a subscription preview, so quantitative detail here is drawn from the abstract plus the corroborating open assessment [[source-gove-1990-assessment]], not from model memory.

Very small linen samples cut in 1988 from a single corner of the Shroud were dated by accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) independently at three laboratories — the University of Arizona, Oxford University, and ETH Zurich — with the British Museum coordinating certification and blind controls (three textiles of independently known age). The abstract states the result plainly: the measurements 'provide conclusive evidence that the linen of the Shroud of Turin is mediaeval.' The three labs' means agree, and the combined calibrated calendar age is AD 1260-1390 at 95% confidence (reported quantitatively in [[source-gove-1990-assessment]]).

This is primary laboratory evidence and the single strongest datum against the claim [[claim-shroud-turin-first-century]]: a 1st-century linen would date roughly AD 1-100, some 1200-1300 years earlier than the measured range. The paper directly refutes the first-century claim and supports a 13th-14th century (medieval) origin. Caveats acknowledged by later work: all subsamples came from one corner, and the twelve subsample results show more scatter than counting statistics predict (see [[source-riani-2013-heterogeneity]]) — the basis of the [[claim-shroud-repair-sample-objection]] sub-claim. Note co-author [[scholar-timothy-jull]] later directly tested the reweave hypothesis in [[source-freer-waters-jull-2026]].
