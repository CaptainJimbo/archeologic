**Peer-reviewed article, Scientific Reports, 2021 (open access).** Fetched via Nature (DOI 10.1038/s41598-021-94859-1); full abstract and introduction returned and ground this note.

Modern primary geoarchaeology confirming and bounding the eruption's reach onto Crete. A geomorphological survey west of the palatial town of **Malia** (north Crete, ~120 km from Santorini) found a unique Late Minoan erosional event capped by a high-energy marine sand unit: a **tsunami that inundated up to ~400 m inland**, reaching the outskirts of the palatial centre. Radiocarbon brackets the event (pre-tsunami 1744-1544 BCE; post-event 1509-1430 BCE).

Two load-bearing quantities temper the popular claim: the reconstructed **run-up did not exceed ~8 m asl**, and Malia was not destroyed by it — the town was abandoned only much later, in **LM IIIB (13th c. BCE)**. So the tsunami impact was real but coastal, localized and survivable, not civilization-ending. The eruption is characterized as VEI 7, ~78-86 km3 DRE — genuinely one of the largest of the Holocene.

The authors explicitly frame the find as opening 'new research on... impact and consequences for the Minoan civilization' — hence it supports the weaker [[claim-thera-indirect-contribution]] while disputing the strong [[claim-thera-destroyed-minoans]]. (Co-author Charlotte Pearson also authored the chronology paper [[source-pearson-2018]].)
