British-born historian of science at Yale who produced the first systematic scientific study of the [[claim-antikythera-astronomical-computer]]. Using early gamma-ray and X-ray radiography of the corroded fragments recovered from [[site-antikythera-wreck]], Price counted the surviving gear teeth and reconstructed gear trains, publishing the landmark monograph Gears from the Greeks (1974). He established that the device was a calendrical/astronomical calculating machine driven by a differential-style gear train, framing it as the ancestor of mechanical clockwork and coining the popular characterization of the mechanism as an ancient computer (analogue and mechanical, not electronic). Later high-resolution imaging by [[scholar-tony-freeth]] and colleagues corrected several of Price's specific gear counts and functions, but confirmed his core thesis that the object is a geared astronomical device.
