**Public-domain pamphlet (fetched this run as full text from the Internet Archive; 1920).** The Norwegian-American scholar Rasmus B. Anderson — first professor of Scandinavian studies in the United States (Wisconsin) and a former U.S. minister to Denmark — sets out his case against the [[claim-kensington-runestone-genuine|Kensington inscription]].

**Stated argument.** Anderson recounts that the scholars who first examined the stone agreed in declaring it a rather clumsy fraud, after which it received little attention and Ohman reused it as a stepping-stone to his granary. He names Olof Ohman and the itinerant ex-clergyman Sven Fogelblad as the likely concocters of the hoax and lists facts he considers conclusive against genuineness, beginning with the date at the end of the inscription. He explicitly rebuts the stone's chief promoter, [[scholar-hjalmar-holand]].

**Bearing on the claim.** An independent early critique from a leading Scandinavian-American scholar; it disputes [[claim-kensington-runestone-genuine]]. It is polemical and partly anecdotal rather than a formal runological study, so it is weighted below the philological analysis of [[source-flom-1910]], but it corroborates that contemporary experts uniformly read the stone as a modern fabrication.
