The minority claim. Jerome Eisenberg (see [[scholar-jerome-eisenberg]]) argued in 2008 that the disk is not a Bronze Age object at all but a hoax made in or about 1908, whether by Luigi Pernier (see [[scholar-luigi-pernier]]) or for him. The case rests on the object's anomalies: it is unique (no second stamped-clay document exists anywhere), its sign edges are unusually crisp, its firing looks deliberate where Minoan clay documents were typically sun-dried, and — the argument's strongest lever — no laboratory test has ever authenticated it.

Why it stays alive rather than settled: the Heraklion museum has declined thermoluminescence testing, which could in principle date the clay's last firing and either confirm a Bronze Age origin or expose a modern one. Absent that test, the forgery hypothesis cannot be laboratory-refuted, so it survives as a genuine, if minority, contest.

Why it is nonetheless held LOW (0.30). Mainstream specialists reject it. The disk was excavated in a documented Middle Minoan deposit on [[site-minoan-crete]] beside independently datable finds, not surface-collected; features such as the sign overcuts (corrections a forger would have no reason to fake) point to genuine scribal use; and no positive evidence of fabrication has surfaced in a century. This node stays CONTESTED, not refuted: the debate is unresolved for want of a lab test, but the evidential balance favours authenticity. Disputes [[claim-phaistos-disc-authentic]].
