**Public-domain scholarly monograph (fetched this run as full text from the Internet Archive; Illinois State Historical Society, 1910).** [[scholar-george-flom]], then of the University of Illinois, gives a philological analysis of the [[claim-kensington-runestone-genuine|Kensington inscription]] under the pointed title A Modern Inscription from Douglas County, Minnesota.

**Stated argument.** Flom notes that the Minnesota Historical Society museum committee which had pronounced the stone genuine included no Scandinavian philologist. Examining the text himself, he concludes that we deal not with a fourteenth-century inscription but with a narrative in a modern dialect recorded in modern runic characters. He shows the vocabulary and grammar to be modern Scandinavian rather than the Old Swedish of 1362 — for instance lager, and the sense journey for resa, are late loans from German absent in the medieval language — and judges the stone a proven forgery.

**Bearing on the claim.** A primary, early, expert linguistic refutation that disputes [[claim-kensington-runestone-genuine]]. Its 1910 date is a strength rather than a weakness: trained philologists rejected the inscription within a decade of its appearance, and later runology has only confirmed the verdict.
