**The claim.** The Bronze Age Linear B tablets from Knossos ([[site-minoan-crete]]), Pylos ([[site-pylos]]) and [[site-mycenae]] are written in an early Greek dialect, now called Mycenaean Greek — the oldest attested form of Greek, c. 1400-1200 BC.

**How it was established.** [[scholar-michael-ventris]], an architect and amateur, in collaboration with the philologist [[scholar-john-chadwick]], announced the decipherment in 1952-53. Their foundational paper ([[source-ventris-chadwick-1953]], JHS) laid out phonetic values for the syllabic signs and showed the resulting words are Greek — a pre-Dorian dialect allied to classical Arcadian and Cypriot. Crucially the decipherment used no bilingual: it worked by internal grid analysis (building on Alice Kober's sign relations) and the reading was later confirmed independently by the Pylos ta-ra-nu / tripod tablet (PY Ta 641) that Blegen found in 1952, whose ideograms match the Greek words the values predicted.

**The overturned prior view.** The excavator of Knossos, [[scholar-arthur-evans]], had insisted Linear B recorded a non-Greek Aegean/Minoan language and that Greeks could not have ruled Knossos — a position that delayed acceptance for decades and that the decipherment reversed. Ventris himself initially suspected the language was Etruscan before the values forced a Greek reading.

**Dissent, and why it failed.** A small number of holdouts objected, most vocally A. J. Beattie of Edinburgh ([[source-beattie-1956]]), who argued the method was circular and the results arbitrary. These objections did not persuade the field: the confirmatory tablets, the coherence of the whole corpus as Greek administrative records, and the successful reading of tablets found *after* the decipherment closed the case.

**Verdict.** Supported — settled scholarly consensus. Mycenaean Greek is a standard object of study; this is one of the rare archaeological claims that is essentially settled-true. Held at 0.92 (reserving a sliver for the acknowledged fact that a syllabary without a bilingual leaves some individual readings debatable, though not the Greek identification itself).
