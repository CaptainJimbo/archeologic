**Open-access full text (fetched this run; PLOS ONE, PMC3566088).** An mitochondrial-DNA study combining hypervariable-region sequences from 14 newly typed Etruscan individuals with other ancient Etruscan and Medieval samples and 4,910 modern Mediterranean individuals, analysed against millions of coalescent computer simulations — an independent, earlier method from the genome-wide transect of [[source-posth-2021]].

**Key findings from the fetched text.** The Etruscans can be considered ancestral, with high confidence, to present-day inhabitants of the Tuscan communities of Casentino and Volterra, but not to the general modern population of the former Etruscan homeland. Critically, the authors estimate that the genetic links between Tuscany and Anatolia date back at least 5,000 years — i.e. to the Neolithic — strongly suggesting that the Etruscan culture developed LOCALLY and not as an immediate consequence of recent immigration from the eastern Mediterranean.

**Provenance note.** This is an independent line of evidence (maternal mtDNA + simulation) reaching the same conclusion as the later genome-wide data, so the refutation of a recent Anatolian migration does not rest on a single study. Any Anatolian genetic signal is deep-time (Neolithic), not Iron Age. This source disputes [[claim-etruscan-anatolian-origin]].
