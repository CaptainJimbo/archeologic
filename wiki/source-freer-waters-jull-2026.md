**Peer-reviewed, open-access article, npj Heritage Science, 18 April 2026.** Full HTML fetched this run via the DOI; abstract and introduction extracted and grounded this note.

The authors physically examine two leftover linen fragments from the very material the University of Arizona cut for the 1988 dating (see [[source-damon-1989-nature]]), comparing them microscopically against the control textiles distributed to the labs. Co-author [[scholar-timothy-jull]] ran the Arizona AMS work in 1988, so this is a direct test of the sampled material itself. The abstract's finding: the analyzed fragments are 'consistent with the main body of the Shroud and show no evidence of contamination' — no cotton, no spliced-in dyed or gummed medieval repair yarn, i.e. no microscopic sign of the 'invisible reweave' proposed to explain a spuriously young date.

This is primary physical evidence bearing directly on [[claim-shroud-repair-sample-objection]]: it disputes the reweave/repair variant of that objection for the actually-dated corner, while the statistical-heterogeneity variant (see [[source-riani-2013-heterogeneity]]) is untouched by it. It therefore reinforces the medieval result and the refutation of [[claim-shroud-turin-first-century]]. Weight it as one line of evidence: it examines small fragments, and reweave proponents dispute whether these came from the exact dated thread.
