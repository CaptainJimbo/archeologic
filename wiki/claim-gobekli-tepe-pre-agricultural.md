Claim: Gobekli Tepe, the monumental T-shaped-pillar complex on the Germus ridge above the Harran plain in southeastern Turkey, was raised by pre-agricultural hunter-gatherer communities, before farming was established in the region.

Verdict: SUPPORTED (confidence ~0.88). The site [[site-gobekli-tepe]] was excavated from 1995 by [[scholar-klaus-schmidt]] and, after his death, [[scholar-oliver-dietrich]] and the wider German Archaeological Institute team. Its enclosures belong to the Pre-Pottery Neolithic A and B (PPNA/PPNB), radiocarbon-dated to roughly 9500-8000 BCE, which is before pottery, before metal, and, in this region, before fully domesticated crops and herd animals. The excavator's first report [[source-schmidt-2000]] places the megaliths and pillars firmly in the PPN. The excavation team's synthesis [[source-dietrich-2012-antiquity]] states outright that the site pushes the origins of monumentality back beyond the emergence of agriculture, describing hunter-gatherers congregating to carve pillars, dance, feast, and brew beer from wild (not domesticated) crops.

Why this matters: the finding overturned the older farming-first model, in which agricultural surplus was thought to be a precondition for monumental architecture and organised cult. At Gobekli Tepe the monuments come first and established farming follows, inverting the assumed causal order.

Nuance and live debate: the strong reading, purely foragers with zero incipient cultivation, is genuinely contested. The same communities processed wild cereals at scale, and neighbouring PPN sites show early plant management, so some researchers frame the builders as very early Neolithic communities on the cusp of cultivation rather than classic Palaeolithic hunter-gatherers. The review entry [[source-peters-2020-domestication]] surveys this agriculture-and-domestication question for the site. This nuance qualifies but does not overturn the core claim: the monuments predate established agriculture and were built by (largely) foraging communities, which is why confidence sits high but short of certainty.
