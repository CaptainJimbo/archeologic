A Roman-era shipwreck off the island of Antikythera between Crete and the Peloponnese, discovered by Symi sponge divers in 1900 and first salvaged in 1901 — one of the earliest underwater archaeology operations. The wreck yielded bronze and marble statuary, glassware, coins and the corroded lumps that proved to contain the geared device now called the Antikythera Mechanism (see [[claim-antikythera-astronomical-computer]]). Ceramic and numismatic finds, together with inscription letter-forms on the mechanism, place the ship's loss in the mid-to-late 1st century BCE, with the mechanism itself generally dated c. 150-100 BCE. The site was reinvestigated by Cousteau (1976) and by ongoing Greek/international expeditions in the 2010s. Primary provenance for [[scholar-derek-de-solla-price]]'s and [[scholar-tony-freeth]]'s study material.
