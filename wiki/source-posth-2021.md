**Open-access full text (fetched this run; Science Advances, PMC8462907).** A genome-wide ancient-DNA time transect of 82 individuals spanning roughly 800 BCE to 1000 CE across Etruria and southern Italy — the largest direct genetic sampling of Etruscan-associated populations to date.

**Key findings from the fetched text.** During the Iron Age the putative non-Indo-European-speaking Etruscans carry a component of Indo-European-associated steppe ancestry and show a LACK of recent Anatolian-related admixture. The authors state that the Etruscan-related gene pool does not seem to have originated from recent population movements from the Near East, and that Etruscans carry a local genetic profile shared with neighbouring populations such as the Latins of Rome and its environs, despite the cultural and linguistic differences between the two groups. The local gene pool is largely maintained across the first millennium BCE; a population-wide shift to about 50 percent eastern-Mediterranean admixture appears only later, in the Roman Imperial period.

**On the language.** The paper explicitly flags that the persistence of a probable non-Indo-European language isolate surrounded by Italic Indo-European-speaking groups such as the Latins is an intriguing and still unexplained phenomenon requiring further archaeological, historical-linguistic, and genetic investigation. Genetics therefore refutes a RECENT demic migration but does not by itself explain the language.
