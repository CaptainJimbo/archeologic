British archaeologist who excavated Knossos on [[site-minoan-crete|Crete]] from 1900 and named the Minoan civilization. He recovered the first large Linear B archive and published the signs (Scripta Minoa), but insisted the script recorded a non-Greek Minoan language and that Knossos was a Minoan, not Mycenaean, seat of power.

His authority entrenched the non-Greek view for a generation and is a textbook case of an eminent excavator's prior commitments delaying a correct reading. The [[claim-linear-b-greek|Ventris-Chadwick decipherment]] overturned Evans's position, showing the tablets are Greek — though the resulting question of who ruled Knossos and when remains debated.
