**The claim as stated.** The traditional explanation, the Dorian Invasion (also called the Return of the Heraclidae), holds that a migration or invasion of Dorian Greeks from the north around 1100 BCE destroyed the Mycenaean palatial world (see [[site-mycenae]]) and plunged Greece into the Greek Dark Age. It was the dominant textbook account for much of the 19th and 20th centuries, resting on much later Greek literary tradition rather than on contemporary Bronze Age evidence.

**Why it is refuted as a cause.** When systematic excavation began, sudden shifts in material culture after the palace destructions seemed to confirm an incoming Dorian people. But in subsequent work a distinct Dorian assemblage never materialised: Catharine Judson 2023 ([[source-judson-2023]], fetched this run) shows for Crete that the Late Bronze Age to Early Iron Age transition is regionally diverse and gradual, not the signature of a single unilinear migration, and that the migration model survives largely as historiographic inertia rather than on positive evidence. The archaeology of the collapse is far better explained as a systems collapse driven by multiple converging stresses ([[claim-lbac-multicausal]]), the same framework that reframes the Sea Peoples as a symptom rather than a cause ([[claim-sea-peoples-collapse]]).

**Population evidence.** A mass invasion that replaced the population should leave a genetic discontinuity. Instead the ancient-DNA study of Lazaridis and colleagues 2017 ([[source-lazaridis-2017]], fetched this run) finds that modern Greeks resemble the Bronze Age Mycenaeans, with only minor later dilution, supporting continuity rather than wholesale replacement. This is indirect for the specific 12th-11th century transition, which that study does not sample directly, but it removes the demographic rupture the invasion narrative implies.

**The kernel of truth.** Something real underlies the tradition: the West Greek (Dorian) dialect did spread, and localized population movements in the postpalatial period are plausible. The philologist John Chadwick ([[scholar-john-chadwick]]) even argued Dorian-speakers were already present within Mycenaean society as a subordinate group, making a dramatic external invasion unnecessary to explain the later dialect map. So a diffuse linguistic and demographic shift is defensible; an organised invasion that *caused* the collapse is not.

**Verdict.** Refuted as stated (the invasion-as-cause claim), held at low confidence 0.22. The collapse is multi-causal; the Dorian Invasion is a late explanatory myth retrofitted onto a more complex, home-grown decline, with at most a modest kernel of real linguistic and population change.
