Gobekli Tepe is a tell on one of the highest points of the Germus mountain range (ca. 770 m asl) overlooking the Harran plain, about 15 km northeast of Sanliurfa in Upper Mesopotamia, southeastern Turkey. The mound is roughly 15 m high and around 300 m across, formed of several knolls divided by hollows and surrounded by a largely soil-free limestone plateau.

First noted as a lithic scatter during the 1960s Istanbul-Chicago survey (site V52/1, reported by Peter Benedict), its megalithic architecture went unrecognised until [[scholar-klaus-schmidt]] identified it in 1994; excavation began in 1995 and continued under [[scholar-oliver-dietrich]] and the German Archaeological Institute after Schmidt's death in 2014.

The site is famous for monumental round-to-oval enclosures, 10-30 m in diameter, ringed by and centred on massive T-shaped limestone pillars, many carved in relief with animals (foxes, snakes, boars, birds, scorpions) and anthropomorphic elements. The architecture belongs to the Pre-Pottery Neolithic A and B and dates to roughly 9500-8000 BCE. It is the type-site for the claim that monumental construction preceded established agriculture: see [[claim-gobekli-tepe-pre-agricultural]].
