Open-access peer-reviewed PLoS ONE article; full text fetched this run via the DOI. Freeth analyses the back-plate Saros Dial, a four-turn spiral on which eclipse events are marked by glyphs describing the type and time of each eclipse, keyed by alphabetical index letters to solar-eclipse inscriptions. He shows the machine predicted eclipses on the basis of the 223-lunar-month Saros cycle and presents two arithmetical models reproducing the glyph distribution, index-letter grouping, and eclipse times, implying a surprisingly early epoch for the mechanism. He notes the predictions were not entirely accurate but an astonishing achievement for the era.
