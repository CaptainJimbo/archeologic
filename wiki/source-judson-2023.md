**Open-access article (fetched this run; Journal of Greek Archaeology, Archaeopress).** A regional case study by Catharine Judson ([[scholar-catharine-judson]], Duke University) on the Early Iron Age of Crete, used to trace how the Dorian invasion narrative arose and why it persists.

**Core argument (from the fetched abstract and references).** When systematic excavation first revealed major shifts in material culture and political structure after the Mycenaean palace destructions, archaeology appeared to confirm the historical reality of a Dorian migration that until then was known only from much later texts. In subsequent excavations a distinct Dorian assemblage failed to materialise, and the Late Bronze Age to Early Iron Age transition proved more complex and regionally diverse than any simple migration model. The mid-20th century rejection of culture-history as a paradigm further undercut using migrations as straightforward explanations for material or linguistic change. Judson notes that a minimally modified Dorian-migration narrative nonetheless retains currency, and that the recent surge of ancient-DNA studies has revived interest in tracing prehistoric mobility.

**Bearing on the claim.** Directly disputes [[claim-dorian-invasion-dark-age]] and supports both [[claim-dorian-no-population-signature]] and the multi-causal collapse framework [[claim-lbac-multicausal]]. This is secondary, review-level scholarship rather than a new excavation report, but it is a recent specialist synthesis of the Cretan evidence and its historiography.
