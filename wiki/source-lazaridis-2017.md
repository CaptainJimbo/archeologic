**Open-access full text (fetched this run; Nature author manuscript in Europe/NCBI PMC, PMC5565772).** Genome-wide ancient DNA from nineteen individuals, including Minoans from Crete, Mycenaeans from mainland Greece, and Bronze Age neighbours from southwestern Anatolia.

**Key findings from the fetched text.** Minoans and Mycenaeans were genetically similar, deriving at least three quarters of their ancestry from the first Neolithic farmers of western Anatolia and the Aegean, with most of the remainder from Caucasus/Iran-related populations. The Mycenaeans differed from Minoans in a further minority component of ancestry related ultimately to hunter-gatherers of eastern Europe and Siberia, introduced via a source related to the Eurasian steppe or Armenia. Crucially, the authors state that modern Greeks resemble the Mycenaeans, with some additional dilution of the early Neolithic ancestry, and that their results support the idea of continuity but not isolation in the populations of the Aegean before and after its earliest civilizations.

**Bearing on the claim.** Primary genetic data. It does not directly sample the 12th-11th century BCE collapse horizon, so it is indirect for the Dorian question, but continuity from the Mycenaeans down to modern Greeks is inconsistent with the mass population replacement that a literal Dorian Invasion implies.
