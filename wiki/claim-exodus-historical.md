Claim as stated: a mass departure of Israelites from Egypt on the biblical scale (Exodus 12:37 gives about 600,000 men on foot, implying a population of roughly two million), followed by a 40-year wilderness wandering and a military conquest of Canaan.

Verdict: refuted at this magnitude, low confidence. The mainstream archaeological assessment (Israel Finkelstein and Neil Asher Silberman, The Bible Unearthed; William G. Dever) finds no archaeological trace of an Israelite population of that size in Egypt, in the Sinai, or along the wilderness route, and no destruction horizon matching a unified conquest. The fetched survey [[source-bosman-2002]] records the same core problem in the scholarly literature: the absence of any trace of Israel as a people in Egypt during the Late Bronze or Early Iron Age. Sites named in the itinerary (for example Kadesh Barnea) show no occupation of the right period at the required scale.

Converging survey evidence instead points to early Israel emerging largely from within Canaan: the Iron Age I highland villages show material continuity with local Canaanite culture rather than an intrusive Egyptian-desert population. The Merneptah Stele (about 1208 BCE) attests a people called Israel already in Canaan, but says nothing of an exodus.

What survives scrutiny is not this large-scale claim but a much weaker version, treated separately as [[claim-exodus-small-kernel]]. The two are in tension: evidence against the mass event does not by itself refute a modest historical seed. Scholars: [[scholar-israel-finkelstein]], [[scholar-william-dever]].
