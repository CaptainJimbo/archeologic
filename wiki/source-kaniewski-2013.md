**Open-access primary study (fetched this run, PLoS ONE, CC-BY).** Palaeoclimate proxy work from Gibala-Tell Tweini on the Syrian coast (kingdom of Ugarit) and from Cyprus, with a radiocarbon-based chronology tying the environmental record to the archaeological horizon of the Late Bronze Age crisis.

**Core finding.** The authors report abrupt climate change to drier conditions across the 13th-9th centuries BC and argue for climate-driven famine as an underlying driver of the crisis. Their framing is explicitly causal-layered: the paper distinguishes the *proximate* cause portrayed by Egyptian and cuneiform sources (the Peoples-of-the-Sea invasions) from the *ultimate* driver (drought and famine).

**Bearing on the Sea Peoples.** Crucially, the paper reframes the Sea Peoples themselves as a *symptom* of the same environmental stress. In their words, the Sea Peoples were not merely pirates pursuing the riches of the Near East but a set of ethnic entities fleeing inhospitable, drought-undermined regions to conquer new lands. This directly undercuts the mono-causal reading in [[claim-sea-peoples-collapse]] and supports the multi-causal reading in [[claim-lbac-multicausal]].

**Caveats.** This is one regional proxy record extrapolated to a pan-Mediterranean event; critics note that a single silver-bullet climate cause is as reductive as a single invasion cause, and that direct causation between climate and societal collapse is hard to prove (a caveat the authors themselves acknowledge). Held as strong but not decisive primary evidence for the environmental strand. Location context: [[site-ugarit]].
