Open-access Scientific Reports paper; full HTML fetched this run via the DOI. Primary materials-science study of how the disc was made, reading the object itself rather than repeating context arguments.

A small piece from the outer part of the disc was studied metallographically (optical microscopy on a colour-etched surface, EDS, electron backscatter diffraction, plus microhardness) and compared against a cast-and-forged replica. The microstructure shows the disc was manufactured from a flat cast followed by a hot-forging process, reheated and forged roughly ten times. The introduction restates the mainstream framing: the disc is the oldest precise depiction of the heavens in human history, was looted by metal detectorists on the Mittelberg near Nebra in 1999 within a hoard of two swords, two flanged axes, two spiral bracelets and a chisel, and the associated finds date the deposition to the end of the central European Early Bronze Age, ca. 1600 BC, with the disc itself likely made some 100 to 200 years earlier and reworked over five phases.

The study assumes and operates within the Early Bronze Age attribution and adds independent manufacturing evidence consistent with EBA sheet-bronze technology; it supports [[claim-nebra-disc-bronze-age]].
