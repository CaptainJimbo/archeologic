**Verdict: REFUTED as stated, confidence 0.15.** The scientific dating evidence places the Shroud's linen in the medieval period, roughly a millennium after the 1st century AD. (This note assesses the physical dating evidence only, not any theological question.)

**Primary evidence.** In 1988 linen samples were dated by accelerator mass spectrometry independently at three laboratories — Arizona, Oxford, and ETH Zurich — coordinated with blind controls by the British Museum, and published as [[source-damon-1989-nature]] (Nature). The combined calibrated result is **AD 1260-1390 at 95% confidence** — medieval, consistent with the cloth's first undisputed appearance in France in the 1350s. A genuine 1st-century linen would date near AD 1-100. The independent assessment [[source-gove-1990-assessment]] by AMS co-inventor Harry Gove judged the result 'credible.' Two independent, converging lines (three-lab AMS agreement; documentary history) both point medieval, so this rests on primary evidence, not citation echo.

**The one real scientific caveat** is the sub-claim [[claim-shroud-repair-sample-objection]]: all samples came from a single corner that shows statistically significant heterogeneity ([[source-riani-2013-heterogeneity]]), and some have argued that corner was a later repair. But the reweave variant is contradicted by direct microscopy of the dated fragments ([[source-freer-waters-jull-2026]]), and no analysis produces a 1st-century date — the objection undermines the sampling's representativeness, not the medieval age of the material measured.

**Confidence 0.15** (not lower) leaves room for the acknowledged sampling imperfection and the theoretical possibility of a future re-dating of multiple, spatially separated sites on the cloth. But on all published scientific dating evidence to date, the 1st-century claim is refuted.
