Open-access peer-reviewed Scientific Reports paper; full HTML fetched this run via the DOI. The abstract calls the Antikythera Mechanism an ancient Greek astronomical calculator, now split into 82 fragments with only about a third surviving including 30 corroded bronze gearwheels, and describes it in the introduction as a mechanical computer of bronze gears that mechanized astronomical cycles and theories. Building on 2005 Microfocus X-ray CT (which decoded the rear), the authors read inscriptions specifying complex planetary periods and propose a new reconstruction of the lost front display as a Cosmos showing the Sun, Moon and all five planets known in antiquity. They characterize it as combining Babylonian period cycles, mathematics from Plato's Academy and Greek astronomical theory. Primary reconstruction from imaging of the artefact; supports the planetary-display aspect of [[claim-antikythera-astronomical-computer]].
