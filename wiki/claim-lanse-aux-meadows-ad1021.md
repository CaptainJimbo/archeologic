**Verdict: SUPPORTED, confidence 0.90.** Three separate pieces of worked wood from Norse contexts at [[site-lanse-aux-meadows]] were each felled in AD 1021.

The result comes from [[source-kuitems-2021]] (Nature). The AD 993 Miyake event — a global single-year spike in atmospheric radiocarbon from a solar particle storm — leaves an unmistakable marker ring; counting tree rings from that ring to each sample's preserved waney (bark) edge yields the exact felling year. All three metal-cut samples gave AD 1021, one with a spring felling season. Because the wood bears clean metal-tool cut marks (Indigenous peoples of the region did not work metal), it is securely Norse.

This sharpens the parent claim [[claim-norse-precolumbian-america]] from 'around AD 1000' to a specific calendar year, exactly 471 years before 1492. It sits comfortably inside the earlier AD 975-1020 radiocarbon window of [[source-nydal-1989]]. Confidence 0.90 rather than higher because AD 1021 dates the felling of three timbers, which need not be the very first or last year of occupation — but it is a firm, independently reproducible tie point.
