Interdisciplinary overview and point-by-point rebuttal, published in Archaeologia Austriaca 104/2020; article landing page and abstract fetched this run via the DOI. It responds directly to the Iron-Age reattribution proposed by Gebhard and Krause (2020), which underlies [[claim-nebra-iron-age-challenge]].

Key points grounded in the fetched text. The authors concede the epistemic weakness at the heart of the case: since the Nebra Sky Disc is a unique find that was not recovered during a controlled excavation, it can neither be dated by traditional typological methods nor prima facie by its appearance, and there is no scientific method yet available to date copper alloys exactly. The Early Bronze Age date was therefore established by reconstructing the find context and by analysing the accompanying finds (two swords, axes, bracelets, a chisel) that are typologically and radiocarbon dated to around 1600 BC.

Against the challenge they argue: the Mittelberg find location was excavated in great detail and multiple scientific analyses confirmed the looters court-trial testimony that the disc had been buried there together with the hoard; these same analyses had earlier disproved the claim that the disc was a modern fake. They state that the Gebhard and Krause argument is based on early published and unpublished material used and cited selectively, ignores a substantial number of subsequent publications, and rests on a distortion of the evidence derived both in the court trial and by scientific analyses, combined with a superficial typological discussion of the imagery. Sediment (corrosion-attachment) analysis, gold chemistry and lead-isotope work are cited as tying the disc to the hoard and the site.
