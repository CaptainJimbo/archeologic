The principal rival to [[claim-tomb-ii-philip-ii]]: the main-chamber male of
[[site-tomb-ii]] is **Philip III Arrhidaios**, Alexander's half-brother and
short-lived successor (died 317 BC), buried with his wife Eurydice. This dates
the tomb roughly two decades later than Philip II's death.

The argument rests on two legs. First, chronology of the grave goods
(some scholars read the pottery and artefacts as post-320 BC). Second, the
forensic claim in [[source-bartsiokas-2000]] that the bones were cremated **dry**
— degreased and unfleshed — implying a body exhumed and burned long after death,
consistent with Arrhidaios's disturbed reburial rather than a fresh royal pyre.

That second leg is exactly what [[claim-tomb-ii-green-cremation]] and
[[source-musgrave-2010]] attack: they argue the bones were burned fleshed, which
undercuts the Arrhidaios reconstruction. Held at 0.45 — the mirror of its rival,
because the debate is unresolved.
