The defensible middle ground left standing once the direct-destruction claim ([[claim-thera-destroyed-minoans]]) is set aside.

**The thesis.** Even though the eruption did not cause the LM IB destructions, its aftermath — crop failure and ashfall on farmland, disrupted maritime trade, damaged coastal harbours from tsunamis, and psychological/religious shock — could have quietly weakened Neopalatial society, so that the richness of LM IB masked underlying economic and political fragility that led to collapse a generation or two later. This is essentially the Driessen & Macdonald *The Troubled Island* argument.

**Evidence for.** The eruption was genuinely enormous (VEI 7, ~78-86 km3 DRE) and its tsunamis reached the north Cretan coast, including the palatial centre of Malia ([[source-lespez-2021]]), whose authors explicitly frame their find as opening 'new research on... impact and consequences for the Minoan civilization.'

**Evidence against / caveats.** The measured impact was modest (run-up not exceeding ~8 m, inundation ~400 m inland) and local to the coast; interior sites show quick rebuilding and a cultural florescence. Subsequent work suggests the 'hidden decline' was not a general island-wide pattern, and separating a real volcanic contribution from ordinary Bronze Age instability is very hard.

Held at 0.45 - genuinely contested: plausible and argued by serious scholars, but under-determined by the evidence and easy to over-read backwards from the known collapse. It disputes the strong claim by offering a weaker, slower causal story rather than endorsing it.
